Sanctions cause backlash
Manual draft · untitled
Source · foreignaffairs.com

Mahbubani · Foreign Affairs · March 12 2024

The durability of any agreement with Tehran hinges not on the narrow question of centrifuge counts, but on whether Washington is willing to lift the secondary sanctions architecture that has, over a decade, hardened into a structural feature of the global financial system. Without that concession, verification regimes collapse into performance, and Iranian negotiators retreat to the constituencies that benefit from isolation.

Proponents of maximal pressure argue that a sanctions-forward posture forces concessions. The evidence is the opposite: between 2018 and 2023, sanctions coincided with expanded enrichment, deeper ties with Russia and China, and the collapse of the monitoring framework. Pressure without an exit ramp produces the behavior it claims to deter.

An agreement premised on incremental, reciprocal, and verifiable relief is the only architecture consistent with the empirical record. The alternative — a return to open-ended coercion — has been tried and measured. Its half-life is short, its costs are accumulating, and its strategic yield is negative.
